    George Magoha Slams Kenyans Opposing CBC, Terms Nelson Havi Incompetent: &quot;Nonesense&quot;

    Education Cabinet Secretary Professor George Magoha has dismissed parents and other stakeholders who have lately been demonising the Competency-Based Curriculum (CBC). The no-nonsense CS said the implementation of the curriculum was not as expensive as its opposers had portrayed it.

    02 Feb 2024, 00:00
    • Education CS George Magoha has come to the defence of the new Competence-Based Curriculum (CBC)
    • According to him, CBC is the best thing happening ever in the Kenyan education system
    • He said much had been achieved so far, looking forward to more success after the transition from the previous curriculum

    Education Cabinet Secretary Professor George Magoha has dismissed parents and other stakeholders who have lately been demonising the Competency-Based Curriculum (CBC).

    The no-nonsense CS said the implementation of the curriculum was not as expensive as its opposers had portrayed it.

    "All this nonsense you are hearing ignore them. I can assure you, in my life, I think the only best thing happening now is the CBC. Let's not allow the busybodies and politicians to divert," he told journalists in Kakamega.

    Since the inception of CBC four years ago, Magoha said much had been achieved and already 90% of the work had been successfully done regarding the transition from the conventional curriculum to the new one.

    At the same time, he took a swipe at the Law Society of Kenya (LSK) president Nelson Havi who had promised to file a petition in court to challenge the implementation of the new curriculum.

    According to Magoha, Havi is unreliable and incompetent and cannot aptly argue on behalf of those having issues with the CBC.

    "Some of the lawyers now can not speak good English. If you listen to them. Me as a common person who did not pass in English can speak better Engliosh than some of the lawyers because they basically cheat in their exams," he said.

    Anti-CBC petition

    Havi had vowed to file a petition challenging CBC after parents decried high costs and inefficiency.

    He argued that education should be cheaper in Kenya when most citizens struggle with low incomes amid the COVID-19 pandemic.

    "I have heard your cries parents, guardians, and teachers. The petition challenging CBC will be filed in court next week. The education system in Kenya should not be an expensive, inefficient and ineffective experiment with our children and their future as is our leadership," he said.

    CBC replaces 8-4-4

    The CBC was launched in Kenya by the Ministry of Education in 2017.

    It was fronted by the Kenya Institute of Curriculum Development (KICD) as the system to replace 8-4-4, which had been criticised for being heavy on theory.

    Yet, students could not transfer the knowledge to real-life situations.

    The idea was to bring up holistic students who are critical thinkers and creators for a better nation in the years to come.

    Even though most parents love the curriculum, according to a TUKO.co.ke poll, they believe it comes with extra financial obligations that some struggle with.

    On every normal school week, a parent is expected to spend some money to acquire materials to aid the students in their homework, expenses that have been added to the school fees.

    Costly books Parents with learners in private schools raised concerns over what they termed as the high cost of books for the CBC pupils in Grade One (1).

    Learners in public schools and private low-cost primary schools are provided with books free of charge by the government.

    But according to parents with learners in private schools who took their frustrations on social media platforms, the prices of the books are costly.
